About this test: In order to translate a mnemonic name (such as "@NETALYZR_DOMAIN@") to an IP address, your computer contacts a Domain Name Service (DNS) resolver. This is a computer controlled by your ISP which acts as an intermediary.

The DNS servers can also answer reverse lookups, translating the IP address to a mnemonic name. Testing whether a reverse lookup succeeds and whether the name obtained in the reverse lookup matches the original name is an important security mechanism, since it provides a degree of assurance that the translation to an IP address was performed correctly. Attackers are often interested in changing DNS information, so that your computer is misdirected.

In this test, your computer looks up the IP addresses of a number of popular domains, and our server performs the corresponding reverse lookups, and checks for consistency. Unfortunately, not all domains support reverse lookups, so sometimes we are unable to find an answer.

What if this test reports a problem: The most common cause is a false positive due to DNS Wildcarding combined with a transient error or incomplete information on our part as to what the proper reverse names may be for the hostname in question.

However, there may be other causes. For example, some ISPs will impersonate www.google.com, resolving it to the address of a local proxy instead of a Google server. There also exists malware which changes victims' DNS settings to employ malicious resolvers. One strand of such malware will cause windowsupdate.microsoft.com to resolve to a Google IP address, or ad.doubleclick.net to resolve anomalously. If our tests indicate such problems, you should update your antivirus software and remove any manual DNS settings on your computer or your NAT.
